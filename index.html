MCQ pearls
  • BP decreases in the 2nd trimester (↓ SVR) — a rise is pathological.
  • Gestational thrombocytopenia: ↓ platelet count is normal; hypercoagulability from ↑ fibrinogen/VII/VIII.
  • Glycosuria is normal (↑GFR exceeds tubular reabsorption) — does not equal GDM.
  • Right-sided hydronephrosis > left: uterine dextrorotation.

Mnemonic: “CROP” — what falls in pregnancy: Creatinine & BUN (↑GFR flushes them out), Resistance (SVR ↓ → BP ↓ in 2nd trim), Oncotic pressure (albumin ↓ → oedema), PaCO₂ (resp. alkalosis) + Protein S (↑VTE risk). Everything else goes up.

Red flags Sinusoidal CTG (smooth wave-like, 2–5 cycles/min, no beat-to-beat variability) = severe fetal hypoxia / anaemia / haemorrhage — high mortality. Pathological CTG = 1 abnormal feature OR 2 non-reassuring features → FBS or expedite delivery.
MCQ pearls
  • Normal baseline FHR 110–160 bpm; variability 5–25 bpm; acceleration ≥15 bpm for ≥15 sec.
  • Acceleration present → fetal acidosis unlikely, even with reduced variability.
  • FBS: normal pH ≥7.25; borderline 7.21–7.24 (repeat in 30 min); abnormal <7.2 → deliver.
  • Partogram alert line = 1 cm/hr dilatation; action line = 4 hours to the right of alert line.
  • Effective contraction: 3/10 min, each lasting 1 min, 50 mmHg intensity; tachysystole = >5 contractions/10 min.
  • Continuous EFM indicated for: oxytocin use, meconium-stained liquor, previous CS, IUGR, multiple gestation, GDM, preeclampsia.
Mnemonic: “HCV” for deceleration causes — Head compression = Early (benign, vagovagal, mirrors contraction); Cord compression = Variable (abrupt, <30 s to nadir, emergent CS if severe); Vasculature (uteroplacental insufficiency) = Late (gradual, >30 s, ominous — FBS or deliver).

MCQ pearls
  • Drugs cross placenta most readily if: lipid-soluble, low MW (<500 D), non-ionised, low protein binding, weakly basic.
  • Insulin = treatment of choice for diabetes in pregnancy; metformin = category B.

  • Amantadine (Cat C) → tetralogy of Fallot / single ventricle with pulmonary atresia.
  • Levothyroxine and folic acid = category A (no fetal risk demonstrated).

Test Yourself — Antenatal Care & Fetal Assessment

Q1. Regarding routine investigations performed at the booking antenatal visit, all of the following are correct EXCEPT:

  1. X-ray pelvimetry is performed routinely in all primigravidas
  2. HBsAg is checked to identify risk of vertical transmission at delivery
  3. Full blood count includes haemoglobin, white cell count, and platelets
  4. Rubella IgG-seronegative women (10–15%) should receive postnatal vaccination
  5. Urine analysis screens for asymptomatic bacteriuria using a midstream sample
Answer: A. X-ray pelvimetry is NOT a routine booking investigation — it is not listed among standard booking investigations in the sheet. All other options (HBsAg, FBC, rubella IgG with postnatal vaccine for seronegatives, and midstream urine for asymptomatic bacteriuria) are explicitly stated as routine booking-visit investigations.

Q2. Regarding haematological changes in normal pregnancy, all of the following are true EXCEPT:

  1. Plasma volume increases by approximately 50%
  2. Red cell mass increases by 20–30%, producing a dilutional fall in haematocrit
  3. Fibrinogen and clotting factors VII and VIII are decreased, increasing bleeding risk
  4. Platelet count may fall slightly (gestational thrombocytopenia)
  5. White blood cell count is increased during pregnancy
Answer: C. The sheet states fibrinogen and factors VII and VIII are increased (hypercoagulable state), while protein S is decreased. This increases thrombosis risk, not bleeding risk. All other options directly reflect the sheet: plasma volume ↑50%, RBC mass ↑20–30% with dilutional anaemia, gestational thrombocytopenia, and leucocytosis are all correct.

Q3. Regarding cardiotocography (CTG), which of the following is FALSE?

  1. The normal baseline fetal heart rate is 110–160 bpm
  2. Normal baseline variability is 5–25 bpm
  3. An acceleration is defined as a rise of ≥15 bpm lasting ≥15 seconds
  4. The presence of accelerations makes fetal acidosis unlikely, even when variability is reduced
  5. Early decelerations are caused by cord compression and require emergent caesarean section
Answer: E. Early decelerations are caused by fetal head compression (vagal response), mirror the contraction exactly, and are benign — no intervention is necessary. Cord compression causes variable decelerations. Options A–D are all correct verbatim statements from the sheet.

Q4. A 30-year-old woman at 34 weeks with a twin pregnancy is planning an international flight of 7 hours. She has no prior history of VTE or thrombophilia. Regarding air travel precautions, which of the following is most appropriate?

  1. Advise her to cancel the flight, as air travel after 32 weeks is absolutely contraindicated in twin pregnancies
  2. Advise properly fitted graduated elastic compression stockings for the flight, regular in-seat calf exercises every 30 minutes, and adequate hydration
  3. Prescribe low-molecular-weight heparin, as this is indicated for all pregnant women on flights over 4 hours
  4. Advise her to reduce fluid intake during the flight to minimise urinary frequency
  5. Reassure her that no specific precautions are needed for flights under 8 hours
Answer: B. For flights >4 hours, the sheet states all pregnant women should wear properly fitted graduated elastic compression stockings; in-seat calf and ankle exercises every 30 minutes and adequate hydration are also recommended. LMWH (C) is only indicated for women with previous VTE, known thrombophilia, morbid obesity, or other significant VTE risk factors — not all pregnant travellers. Reducing fluid intake (D) is explicitly wrong — the sheet says maintain hydration and avoid caffeine/alcohol. Air travel in high-risk pregnancies (including multiple gestation) is discouraged after 32 weeks (A), but this is not an absolute contraindication; the woman still needs precautions, not cancellation.

Q5. A primigravida at 40 weeks is in active labour. Her cervix was 4 cm dilated on admission. Four hours later, repeat examination shows 5 cm dilatation. The CTG is normal. Contractions are 2 in 10 minutes. On the partogram, the dilatation plot has crossed the alert line. What is the most appropriate plan of management?

  1. Proceed immediately to caesarean section, as the action line has been crossed
  2. No action needed — re-evaluate in 4 hours as per standard primigravida schedule
  3. Perform fetal blood sampling to assess pH before any intervention
  4. Check vital signs, reassess contraction frequency, and consider oxytocin augmentation and/or IV fluids; re-evaluate every 1–2 hours
  5. Deliver by instrumental delivery as the head is engaged and she is in the second stage
Answer: D. The sheet states that when the dilatation plot crosses the alert line (1 cm/hr rate not maintained), the correct response is to check vital signs, assess contractions, consider oxytocin/IV fluids/analgesia, and re-evaluate every 1–2 hours in the first stage. Caesarean section (A) is only triggered if the action line (4 hours to the right of the alert line) is crossed and the patient is not fully dilated. The alert and action lines are distinct — crossing the alert line alone does not mandate delivery.

bleeding and pain in the first trimester — when it is benign and when it is dangerous

MCQ pearls
  • MC cause of 1st-trimester abortion: chromosomal abnormality (50–70%; mostly trisomy/triploidy/monosomy).
  • MC cause of 2nd-trimester abortion: cervical incompetence.
  • Blighted ovum (anembryonic pregnancy): gestational sac >20 mm, no yolk sac/fetal pole on TV-US ×2 ≥7 days apart after 7 weeks.
  • Misoprostol (PGE1, Cytotec) = medical option for missed abortion in 1st and 2nd trimesters; main SE: nausea, vomiting, fever.
  • Recurrent abortion idiopathic (50%): with supportive care alone, 60–70% achieve successful pregnancy.
  • Anti-D: give to all Rh−, non-sensitised women with incomplete/inevitable/missed/septic abortion; not required for complete or threatened <12 w.
Mnemonic: “Closed = Calm, Open = Act” — Closed cervix: Threatened (fetal heart present → reassure) or Complete (empty cavity → reassure) or Missed (no heart activity → D&C/misoprostol); Open (dilated) cervix: Inevitable, Incomplete, or Septic → all need resuscitation + evacuation (Septic: antibiotics first, 12 h then evacuate).

Ectopic Pregnancy

Definition Implantation of a fertilised ovum outside the uterine cavity; accounts for 1–2% of all pregnancies and remains the leading cause of 1st-trimester pregnancy-related death.
Feature Unruptured Ectopic Ruptured Ectopic
Haemodynamics Stable Unstable (shock, tachycardia, ↓BP)

Red flags Any woman of reproductive age with pain + vaginal bleeding → exclude ectopic. Discriminatory zone: β-hCG ≥1000–1500 mIU/mL (TV-US) or ≥6000 mIU/mL (TA-US) — no IUP visible = ectopic until proven otherwise.
MCQ pearls
  • MC site: ampulla of fallopian tube (80%); earliest to rupture: isthmus.
  • Strongest RF: previous ectopic pregnancy (1 in 3 subsequent pregnancies may also be ectopic).
  • PID (MC organism: Chlamydia trachomatis): 1 episode → 13% tubal damage; 3 episodes → 75%.
  • MTX contraindications: β-hCG >5000, fetal cardiac activity, free fluid on U/S, hepatic/renal/haematologic dysfunction.
  • MTX success marker: β-hCG falls ≥15% from day 4 to day 7; initial rise in first 1–3 days is normal — do not alarm.
  • Progesterone <5 ng/mL = non-viable pregnancy (ectopic or failed IUP) with 100% sensitivity; >25 ng/mL suggests viable IUP.
Mnemonic: “5 is the MTX limit” — MTX is safe when β-hCG <5000, sac ≤4 cm, no cardiac activity, no free fluid, patient stable. Any one of those flips → go straight to surgery. MTX success = β-hCG drops ≥15% from day 4→7 (ignore the day 1–3 rise).

Red flags Classic triad: pregnancy <20 w + HTN/proteinuria (pre-eclampsia before 20 w) + vaginal passage of vesicles / no fetal heart tones. Post-molar GTN diagnosis: β-hCG plateau ×4 measurements over 3 weeks OR rise ×3 measurements over 2 weeks OR elevated ≥6 months.
MCQ pearls
  • MC presenting symptom of complete HM: vaginal bleeding (97%); large-for-dates uterus in 50%.
  • Treatment: suction curettage (preferred, preserves fertility); hysterectomy only if sterilisation desired.
  • β-hCG follow-up: weekly until normal ×3 consecutive weeks → monthly; benign = 6 months, single-agent = 12 months, combination = 24 months.
  • FIGO score ≤6 = low risk → single-agent chemo (MTX-FA); ≥7 = high risk → combination (EMA-CO).
  • PSTT: low β-hCG, high HPL; surgery is primary treatment; can follow any type of pregnancy.
  • Choriocarcinoma: 50% follow HM; MC mets = lung (80%) → then vagina (30%), brain (10%), liver (10%).
Mnemonic: “Complete = ALL Dad's, Partial = triploid lad” — Complete: 46XX, ALL chromosomes paternal (androgenic), no fetus, snowstorm on U/S, large uterus, 15–20% malignant risk; Partial: 69XXY (triploid — one extra set), fetus often present, small uterus, mimics missed abortion, only 0.5–1% malignant risk (Anti-D given for partial only).

Thrombophilia & Recurrent Miscarriage

Definition Increased tendency to form clots; in pregnancy, thrombophilia exaggerates the physiological hypercoagulable state → placental microthrombi → impaired trophoblast invasion → recurrent miscarriage, IUGR, stillbirth, PET.
Feature Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS) Inherited Thrombophilia (e.g., FVL, Protein C/S deficiency)
Type Acquired Hereditary (15% of western population)
Antibodies Lupus anticoagulant + anticardiolipin Ab → false +ve VDRL, falsely prolonged PTT Protein C & S deficiency, AT III deficiency, Factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A
Sapporo criteria (obstetric) ≥3 miscarriages <10 w OR ≥1 fetal death ≥10 w OR ≥1 premature birth <34 w (severe PET/placental insuff.) Recurrent miscarriage more in 2nd trimester; FVL → late preterm (vs early in APS)

Red flags Pregnancy: physiological ↑ factors VIII/IX/X/fibrinogen + ↓ protein S + ↓ AT III → normal hypercoagulable state; thrombophilia overshoots this. PE = major cause of maternal mortality (fatal in 15%; 66% within 30 min). LMWH must be stopped 12 hours before induction or epidural.
MCQ pearls
  • APS gold standard treatment: aspirin + LMWH; significantly improves live birth rate over either alone.
  • Recurrent miscarriage defined as ≥3 consecutive losses; idiopathic = MC cause (~50%); cervical cerclage best done at 14 weeks for incompetent cervix.
  • DVT in pregnancy: more commonly proximal, left lower limb (May-Thurner mechanism — compression of L iliac vein by R iliac artery, worsened by gravid uterus).
  • LMWH (enoxaparin) does not cross the placenta — safe for fetus; warfarin allowed postpartum only.
  • Most inherited thrombophilias do NOT require treatment unless: past VTE, homozygous FVL, prothrombin mutation, or antithrombin deficiency.
  • Late pregnancy loss (12–20 w) suggests hypercoagulable state (APS, FVL, protein S deficiency) or cervical insufficiency; early loss (<12 w) favours chromosomal cause.
Mnemonic: “APS = Aspirin + heParin Standard” — gold standard for APS in pregnancy is Aspirin 75 mg/day + Prophylactic LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SC); together they Significantly improve live birth rate over either alone. Remember: LMWH does not cross the placenta; stop it 12 h before induction/epidural; continue up to 6 weeks postpartum.

Q1. Regarding the management of septic abortion, all of the following are correct EXCEPT:

  1. It is defined as an incomplete abortion complicated by infection of the uterine contents.
  2. IV cephalosporin combined with IV metronidazole is the antibiotic regimen of choice.
  3. Surgical evacuation of the uterus should be performed immediately upon diagnosis before antibiotic levels have been established.

Answer: C. Surgical evacuation is performed approximately 12 hours after starting antibiotics — only after a reasonable tissue level has been achieved. Immediate evacuation before antibiotic coverage is incorrect.

Q2. Causes of second-trimester miscarriage include all of the following EXCEPT:

  1. Cervical incompetence.
  2. Multiple pregnancy.
  3. Uterine anomalies and submucous fibroid.
  4. Chromosomal abnormalities of the fetus.
  5. Genital tract infection and premature rupture of membranes.
Answer: D. Fetal chromosomal abnormalities are the MC cause of first-trimester abortion (50–70%). Second-trimester losses are caused by cervical incompetence, multiple pregnancy, uterine anomalies, fibroids, and genital tract infection/PROM.

Q3. A 29-year-old woman at 11 weeks presents with mild vaginal bleeding and passage of small tissue fragments. Uterine size is small for dates. TV-US shows a fetal pole and focal, variable villous swelling. Histological review of the curettings is most likely to reveal a karyotype of:

  1. 69XXY.
  2. 46XX with both chromosomes of paternal origin.
  3. 45X0.
  4. 46XX with one maternal and one paternal X chromosome.
  5. Trisomy 16.
Answer: A. The clinical picture — small-for-dates uterus, fetal pole present, focal villous swelling mimicking missed/incomplete abortion — is characteristic of a partial hydatidiform mole, whose karyotype is triploid, most commonly 69XXY.

Q4. Regarding methotrexate therapy for ectopic pregnancy, all of the following are contraindications EXCEPT:

  2. Serum β-hCG level of 6 200 mIU/mL.

Answer: E. Option E meets all criteria for MTX: haemodynamically stable, β-hCG <5000 mIU/mL, sac ≤4 cm, no cardiac activity. A β-hCG >5000 (B), fetal cardiac activity (A), free fluid (C), and active peptic ulcer (D) are all listed contraindications.

Q5. A 34-year-old woman has had four consecutive pregnancy losses — three under 9 weeks and one unexplained fetal death at 12 weeks of a morphologically normal fetus. Q2. A 32-year-old woman with a satisfactory colposcopy has a directed punch biopsy from an ectocervical lesion. ECC is negative. Histology returns CIN III. She is a reliable patient. What is the most appropriate management?

  1. Immediate LEEP/laser/cryotherapy followed by follow-up
  2. Repeat punch biopsy in 6 months
  3. Cone biopsy because ECC is required to be positive first
  4. Follow-up cytology in 12 months as the patient is reliable
  5. Radical trachelectomy to preserve fertility
Answer: A. CIN II/III requires immediate ablative or excisional treatment (LEEP/laser/cryotherapy) regardless of reliability — watchful follow-up is only acceptable for CIN I in a reliable patient.

Q3. Regarding endometrial hyperplasia and cancer, which ONE of the following statements is INCORRECT?

  1. 75% of endometrial carcinomas occur in postmenopausal women
  2. Type 1 endometrial carcinoma is oestrogen-dependent and usually well-differentiated
  3. Stage is the most important prognostic factor in endometrial cancer
  4. Combined oral contraceptive pills and progesterone are protective against endometrial cancer
  5. Complex hyperplasia without atypia carries a 30% risk of progression to carcinoma
Answer: E. It is complex hyperplasia with atypia that carries a 30% risk of progression — complex hyperplasia without atypia carries only 3%.

Q4. A 26-year-old woman is found to have a right ovarian mass. Her serum LDH is markedly elevated; AFP and CA-125 are normal. The mass is solid and she has no ascites. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Serous cystadenocarcinoma
  2. Yolk sac (endodermal sinus) tumour
  3. Granulosa cell tumour
  4. Dysgerminoma
  5. Choriocarcinoma of germ cell origin
Answer: D. Dysgerminoma is the most common malignant germ cell tumour; its marker is LDH. Yolk sac tumour (option B) secretes AFP; choriocarcinoma (option E) secretes HCG.

Q5. Regarding gynaecological surgical procedures, all of the following are correct EXCEPT:

  1. The most common therapeutic indication for D&C is removal of retained products of conception
  2. Asherman's syndrome is a chronic complication of excessive curettage
  3. Cervical incompetence is a chronic complication of excessive dilatation during D&C
  4. Hysteroscopy can assess adenomyosis and tubal pathology in addition to the endometrial cavity
  5. Vaginal hysterectomy is associated with shorter hospital stay and faster recovery than the abdominal route
Answer: D. Hysteroscopy cannot assess adenomyosis, tubal pathology, or the external uterine contour — it visualises only the endometrial cavity, tubal ostia, and endocervical canal.
