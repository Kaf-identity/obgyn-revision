Physiological Changes in Pregnancy

Definition Coordinated maternal adaptations across all organ systems to support fetoplacental growth; most resolve postpartum.

RespiratoryTidal volume ↑; minute ventilation ↑40%; PaCO₂ ↓ (resp. alkalosis); FRC ↓

MCQ pearls
  • BP decreases in the 2nd trimester (↓ SVR) — a rise is pathological.
  • Gestational thrombocytopenia: ↓ platelet count is normal; hypercoagulability from ↑ fibrinogen/VII/VIII.
  • Glycosuria is normal (↑GFR exceeds tubular reabsorption) — does not equal GDM.
  • Right-sided hydronephrosis > left: uterine dextrorotation.
  • Fluorinated steroids (betamethasone, dexamethasone) cross placenta — used for fetal lung maturation; hydrocortisone 90% inactivated by placenta.
Mnemonic: “CROP” — what falls in pregnancy: Creatinine & BUN (↑GFR flushes them out), Resistance (SVR ↓ → BP ↓ in 2nd trim), Oncotic pressure (albumin ↓ → oedema), PaCO₂ (resp. alkalosis) + Protein S (↑VTE risk). Everything else goes up.

  • LMP dating assumes 28-day cycle, ovulation day 14; inaccurate with lactation, irregular cycles, OCP use in past 3 months.

Red flags Sinusoidal CTG (smooth wave-like, 2–5 cycles/min, no beat-to-beat variability) = severe fetal hypoxia / anaemia / haemorrhage — high mortality. Pathological CTG = 1 abnormal feature OR 2 non-reassuring features → FBS or expedite delivery.
MCQ pearls
  • Normal baseline FHR 110–160 bpm; variability 5–25 bpm; acceleration ≥15 bpm for ≥15 sec.
  • Acceleration present → fetal acidosis unlikely, even with reduced variability.
  • FBS: normal pH ≥7.25; borderline 7.21–7.24 (repeat in 30 min); abnormal <7.2 → deliver.
  • Partogram alert line = 1 cm/hr dilatation; action line = 4 hours to the right of alert line.
  • Effective contraction: 3/10 min, each lasting 1 min, 50 mmHg intensity; tachysystole = >5 contractions/10 min.
  • Continuous EFM indicated for: oxytocin use, meconium-stained liquor, previous CS, IUGR, multiple gestation, GDM, preeclampsia.
Mnemonic: “HCV” for deceleration causes — Head compression = Early (benign, vagovagal, mirrors contraction); Cord compression = Variable (abrupt, <30 s to nadir, emergent CS if severe); Vasculature (uteroplacental insufficiency) = Late (gradual, >30 s, ominous — FBS or deliver).

Test Yourself — Antenatal Care & Fetal Assessment

Q1. A 28-year-old G3P1+1 attends her booking visit at 10 weeks. Her LMP was 1 March 2025. What is her expected date of delivery?

  1. 1 December 2025
  2. 8 December 2025
  3. 15 December 2025
  4. 1 January 2026
  5. 22 November 2025
Answer: B. EDD = LMP + 9 months + 7 days → 1 March + 9 months = 1 December + 7 days = 8 December 2025. Option A misses the 7-day addition; option C adds 14 days instead of 7.

Q2. A 32-year-old primigravida at 30 weeks reports progressively worsening heartburn and ankle swelling. Her blood pressure is 118/74 mmHg. Which physiological change best explains both symptoms?

  1. Increased cardiac output
  2. Increased GFR with glycosuria
  3. Decreased lower oesophageal sphincter tone and decreased serum albumin
  4. Increased tidal volume with respiratory alkalosis
  5. Increased progesterone causing smooth muscle relaxation and sodium retention
Answer: C. ↓ LES tone causes GERD; ↓ albumin lowers oncotic pressure → peripheral oedema (>50% of pregnant women). Increased CO (A) does not directly cause either; glycosuria (B) is unrelated; respiratory alkalosis (D) causes neither.

Q3. On CTG at 38 weeks during active labour, you note recurrent decelerations that begin abruptly, reach their nadir in <30 seconds, last 80 seconds, and are biphasic with loss of variability within the deceleration. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Expedite delivery — emergent caesarean section
  2. No intervention — these are benign early decelerations
  3. Commence conservative measures and repeat CTG in 20 minutes
  4. Perform fetal blood sampling for pH
  5. Administer IV oxytocin to shorten labour and reduce exposure to decelerations
Answer: A. These are severe variable decelerations (>60 s, biphasic, ↓ variability within decel) — concerning characteristics signalling cord compression requiring emergent CS. B is wrong because early decels mirror contractions and are benign. FBS (D) is indicated for late decelerations, not severe variable decelerations with these features.

Q4. A 26-year-old woman at 8 weeks gestation is found to have a mechanical heart valve and needs anticoagulation. She asks about warfarin. Which statement is most accurate?

  1. Warfarin is category B and safe throughout pregnancy
  2. Heparin is more effective than warfarin for thromboembolism
  3. Low-molecular-weight heparin is category C and crosses the placenta
  4. Warfarin is category X but may be used in mechanical heart valves as the only indication in pregnancy
  5. Warfarin should be replaced with aspirin 75 mg/day throughout pregnancy in all patients with mechanical heart valves
Answer: D. Warfarin is FDA category X (nasal hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses, MR in 1st trim; microcephaly, haemorrhage in 2nd–3rd trim). The sheet states the only indication to use it in pregnancy is mechanical heart valves with high thromboembolism risk. Heparin does NOT cross the placenta and is less effective than warfarin (B wrong); LMWH is category B (C wrong).

Q5. A 24-year-old woman with epilepsy plans her first pregnancy and asks about her anticonvulsant. Which drug is most strongly associated with neural tube defects by reducing folate reabsorption?

  1. Carbamazepine
  2. Phenytoin
  3. Valproic acid
  4. Benzodiazepines
  5. Lamotrigine
Answer: C. Valproic acid (Cat D) causes neural tube defects specifically by ↓ folate reabsorption, plus cognitive impairment and autism risk. Phenytoin/carbamazepine (A, B) cause fetal hydantoin syndrome (craniofacial defects, IUGR, nail hypoplasia) — not NTDs. Benzodiazepines (D) cause neonatal respiratory depression and craniofacial anomalies.

bleeding and pain in the first trimester — when it is benign and when it is dangerous

Miscarriage (Types & Management)

MCQ pearls
  • MC cause of 1st-trimester abortion: chromosomal abnormality (50–70%; mostly trisomy/triploidy/monosomy).
  • MC cause of 2nd-trimester abortion: cervical incompetence.
  • Blighted ovum (anembryonic pregnancy): gestational sac >20 mm, no yolk sac/fetal pole on TV-US ×2 ≥7 days apart after 7 weeks.
  • Misoprostol (PGE1, Cytotec) = medical option for missed abortion in 1st and 2nd trimesters; main SE: nausea, vomiting, fever.
  • Recurrent abortion idiopathic (50%): with supportive care alone, 60–70% achieve successful pregnancy.
  • Anti-D: give to all Rh−, non-sensitised women with incomplete/inevitable/missed/septic abortion; not required for complete or threatened <12 w.
Mnemonic: “Closed = Calm, Open = Act” — Closed cervix: Threatened (fetal heart present → reassure) or Complete (empty cavity → reassure) or Missed (no heart activity → D&C/misoprostol); Open (dilated) cervix: Inevitable, Incomplete, or Septic → all need resuscitation + evacuation (Septic: antibiotics first, 12 h then evacuate).

Ectopic Pregnancy

Definition Implantation of a fertilised ovum outside the uterine cavity; accounts for 1–2% of all pregnancies and remains the leading cause of 1st-trimester pregnancy-related death.
Feature Unruptured Ectopic Ruptured Ectopic
Haemodynamics Stable Unstable (shock, tachycardia, ↓BP)
Abdomen Mild tenderness ± adnexal mass Board-like rigidity, rebound, guarding
β-hCG <5000 mIU/mL (suitable for MTX) Any level; decision is surgical

Red flags Any woman of reproductive age with pain + vaginal bleeding → exclude ectopic. Discriminatory zone: β-hCG ≥1000–1500 mIU/mL (TV-US) or ≥6000 mIU/mL (TA-US) — no IUP visible = ectopic until proven otherwise.
MCQ pearls
  • MC site: ampulla of fallopian tube (80%); earliest to rupture: isthmus.
  • Strongest RF: previous ectopic pregnancy (1 in 3 subsequent pregnancies may also be ectopic).
  • PID (MC organism: Chlamydia trachomatis): 1 episode → 13% tubal damage; 3 episodes → 75%.
  • MTX contraindications: β-hCG >5000, fetal cardiac activity, free fluid on U/S, hepatic/renal/haematologic dysfunction.
  • MTX success marker: β-hCG falls ≥15% from day 4 to day 7; initial rise in first 1–3 days is normal — do not alarm.
  • Progesterone <5 ng/mL = non-viable pregnancy (ectopic or failed IUP) with 100% sensitivity; >25 ng/mL suggests viable IUP.
Mnemonic: “5 is the MTX limit” — MTX is safe when β-hCG <5000, sac ≤4 cm, no cardiac activity, no free fluid, patient stable. Any one of those flips → go straight to surgery. MTX success = β-hCG drops ≥15% from day 4→7 (ignore the day 1–3 rise).

Molar Pregnancy / GTD

Red flags Classic triad: pregnancy <20 w + HTN/proteinuria (pre-eclampsia before 20 w) + vaginal passage of vesicles / no fetal heart tones. Post-molar GTN diagnosis: β-hCG plateau ×4 measurements over 3 weeks OR rise ×3 measurements over 2 weeks OR elevated ≥6 months.
MCQ pearls
  • MC presenting symptom of complete HM: vaginal bleeding (97%); large-for-dates uterus in 50%.
  • Treatment: suction curettage (preferred, preserves fertility); hysterectomy only if sterilisation desired.
  • β-hCG follow-up: weekly until normal ×3 consecutive weeks → monthly; benign = 6 months, single-agent = 12 months, combination = 24 months.
  • FIGO score ≤6 = low risk → single-agent chemo (MTX-FA); ≥7 = high risk → combination (EMA-CO).
  • PSTT: low β-hCG, high HPL; surgery is primary treatment; can follow any type of pregnancy.
  • Choriocarcinoma: 50% follow HM; MC mets = lung (80%) → then vagina (30%), brain (10%), liver (10%).
Mnemonic: “Complete = ALL Dad's, Partial = triploid lad” — Complete: 46XX, ALL chromosomes paternal (androgenic), no fetus, snowstorm on U/S, large uterus, 15–20% malignant risk; Partial: 69XXY (triploid — one extra set), fetus often present, small uterus, mimics missed abortion, only 0.5–1% malignant risk (Anti-D given for partial only).

Thrombophilia & Recurrent Miscarriage

Definition Increased tendency to form clots; in pregnancy, thrombophilia exaggerates the physiological hypercoagulable state → placental microthrombi → impaired trophoblast invasion → recurrent miscarriage, IUGR, stillbirth, PET.
Feature Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS) Inherited Thrombophilia (e.g., FVL, Protein C/S deficiency)
Type Acquired Hereditary (15% of western population)
Antibodies Lupus anticoagulant + anticardiolipin Ab → false +ve VDRL, falsely prolonged PTT Protein C & S deficiency, AT III deficiency, Factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A
Sapporo criteria (obstetric) ≥3 miscarriages <10 w OR ≥1 fetal death ≥10 w OR ≥1 premature birth <34 w (severe PET/placental insuff.) Recurrent miscarriage more in 2nd trimester; FVL → late preterm (vs early in APS)

Red flags Pregnancy: physiological ↑ factors VIII/IX/X/fibrinogen + ↓ protein S + ↓ AT III → normal hypercoagulable state; thrombophilia overshoots this. PE = major cause of maternal mortality (fatal in 15%; 66% within 30 min). LMWH must be stopped 12 hours before induction or epidural.
MCQ pearls
  • APS gold standard treatment: aspirin + LMWH; significantly improves live birth rate over either alone.
  • Recurrent miscarriage defined as ≥3 consecutive losses; idiopathic = MC cause (~50%); cervical cerclage best done at 14 weeks for incompetent cervix.
  • DVT in pregnancy: more commonly proximal, left lower limb (May-Thurner mechanism — compression of L iliac vein by R iliac artery, worsened by gravid uterus).
  • LMWH (enoxaparin) does not cross the placenta — safe for fetus; warfarin allowed postpartum only.
  • Most inherited thrombophilias do NOT require treatment unless: past VTE, homozygous FVL, prothrombin mutation, or antithrombin deficiency.
  • Late pregnancy loss (12–20 w) suggests hypercoagulable state (APS, FVL, protein S deficiency) or cervical insufficiency; early loss (<12 w) favours chromosomal cause.
Mnemonic: “APS = Aspirin + heParin Standard” — gold standard for APS in pregnancy is Aspirin 75 mg/day + Prophylactic LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SC); together they Significantly improve live birth rate over either alone. Remember: LMWH does not cross the placenta; stop it 12 h before induction/epidural; continue up to 6 weeks postpartum.

Test Yourself — Early Pregnancy Complications

Q1. A 28-year-old G2P1 presents at 8 weeks with mild vaginal spotting. Pelvic exam shows a closed cervix and appropriately-sized uterus. TV-US confirms fetal cardiac activity. What is the most appropriate next step?

  1. Immediate suction curettage
  2. Administer misoprostol orally
  3. Reassurance, reduce physical activity, and follow up as high-risk pregnancy
  4. Start IV antibiotics and schedule evacuation in 12 hours
  5. Administer intramuscular progesterone to prevent further bleeding
Answer: C. This is a threatened abortion — closed cervix, fetal heart activity present, mild bleeding. Management is reassurance + reduce activity (>90% progress satisfactorily); D is for septic abortion.

Q2. A 24-year-old woman with 7 weeks amenorrhoea presents to the ER with sudden severe right iliac fossa pain and shoulder-tip pain. BP 90/60, HR 118. Serum β-hCG is 8200 mIU/mL. TV-US shows no IUP and free fluid in the pouch of Douglas. Best next step?

  1. Methotrexate 1 mg/kg IM immediately
  2. Expectant management with serial β-hCG
  3. Dilatation and curettage to confirm absence of chorionic villi
  4. Emergency surgical exploration (laparoscopy/laparotomy)
  5. IV fluid resuscitation only; recheck β-hCG in 48 hours to confirm ectopic
Answer: D. Ruptured ectopic pregnancy — haemodynamic instability + free fluid = surgical emergency. MTX (A) is absolutely contraindicated when the patient is unstable and free fluid is present.

Q3. A 32-year-old G1P0 presents at 14 weeks with heavy vaginal bleeding. Uterus is large for dates. β-hCG is markedly elevated. BP 148/95 mmHg. TV-US shows a snowstorm pattern with no fetal parts. Karyotype of the tissue is most likely to show:

  1. 69XXY
  2. 46XX with both chromosomes of paternal origin
  3. 45X0
  4. Trisomy 21
  5. 46XY with one maternal and one paternal chromosome
Answer: B. Classic complete hydatidiform mole — 46XX, androgenic (both X chromosomes paternally derived). 69XXY (A) is partial mole, which typically mimics missed abortion and is small for dates.

Q4. A 30-year-old with three consecutive 1st-trimester miscarriages is found to have lupus anticoagulant and elevated anticardiolipin IgG antibodies on two separate occasions 12 weeks apart. She has no prior thrombotic events. Most appropriate treatment in her next pregnancy?

  1. Low-dose aspirin alone (75 mg/day)
  2. Warfarin throughout pregnancy
  3. Low-dose aspirin 75 mg/day + LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily)
  4. No pharmacological treatment; supportive care only
  5. Prednisolone 5 mg/day + low-dose aspirin to suppress the autoimmune response
Answer: C. This is APS (Sapporo: ≥3 miscarriages <10 w + positive antibodies). Gold standard = aspirin + LMWH, which significantly improves live birth rate over either alone. Warfarin (B) is teratogenic and contraindicated in pregnancy.

Q5. A 26-year-old woman receives a single dose of methotrexate for an unruptured ectopic pregnancy (β-hCG 3200 mIU/mL, sac 3 cm, no cardiac activity). On day 3 post-injection her β-hCG has risen from 3200 to 3500 mIU/mL. She is haemodynamically stable and asymptomatic. What is the correct interpretation and action?

  1. Treatment failure — proceed to immediate surgery
  2. Normal expected response — recheck β-hCG on day 7 and look for ≥15% fall from day 4 to day 7
  3. Give a second dose of methotrexate immediately on day 3
  4. Admit for observation and start IV antibiotics
  5. Obtain urgent TV-US; the rising β-hCG confirms cardiac activity has developed and surgery is now mandatory
Answer: B. An initial rise in β-hCG in the first 1–3 days after MTX is normal and not cause for alarm. The monitoring protocol requires repeat levels on day 4 and day 7; a fall of ≥15% from day 4 to day 7 confirms adequate response.

  • Effective contraction (active phase): 3 contractions/10 min, intensity >50 mmHg, duration 1 min; >5 = hypertonic.

  • Most effective pain relief in labour: epidural (requires Foley catheter to prevent urinary retention).

  • Osteoporosis: BMD ≥2.5 SD below young-adult mean = osteoporosis; early sign = loss of height >1.5 inches (3.8 cm) from vertebral compression; screen with DEXA; 1st-line = bisphosphonates.
  • CVD: early menopause without estrogen = 2–4× ↑ CHD risk; ↑ LDL/total cholesterol, ↓ HDL — reversed by estrogen.
  • Collagen loss: 30% lost in first 5 years → wrinkling, ↓ bone density.
  • Obese women have fewer vasomotor symptoms (peripheral estrone production); long-term complications more severe in premature menopause.
Red flags Loss of height >1.5 inches (vertebral fracture); postmenopausal bleeding (investigate endometrium — TVUS >4–5 mm suspicious); FSH >40 mIU/mL <40 y on two tests 1 month apart = POI.
Mnemonic: Menopausal symptoms — “VAMPS”: Vasomotor (hot flushes, night sweats — hypothalamic instability from E deficiency), Atrophy — urogenital (vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTIs), Mood & cognition (depression, anxiety, poor memory), Psychological & sleep (insomnia, low libido), Skeletal & skin (osteoporosis, joint pain, collagen loss, wrinkling).

Test Yourself — Menopause & HRT

Q1. A 52-year-old woman reports 12 months of amenorrhea, daily hot flushes lasting 3 minutes each, and vaginal dryness. She has an intact uterus. Which HRT regimen is most appropriate?

  1. Estrogen-only oral conjugated equine estrogen 0.625 mg daily
  2. Raloxifene 60 mg daily
  3. Combined continuous estrogen + progestogen
  4. Tibolone alone without progestogen monitoring
  5. Transdermal estrogen patch alone (no progestogen needed as it avoids first-pass metabolism)
Answer: C. Intact uterus requires progestogen alongside estrogen to prevent endometrial hyperplasia/cancer. Estrogen-only (A) is safe only after hysterectomy. Raloxifene (B) does not treat vasomotor symptoms. Tibolone (D) has intrinsic progestogenic activity but still carries endometrial cancer risk if used improperly.

Q2. A 38-year-old woman is found to have FSH 48 mIU/mL confirmed on repeat testing 5 weeks later, with low estradiol and 6 months of amenorrhea. What is the diagnosis?

  1. Early ovarian aging
  2. Physiological perimenopause
  3. Hypothalamic amenorrhea
  4. Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)
  5. Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
Answer: D. POI = amenorrhea >4 months + FSH >40 mIU/mL on two occasions 1 month apart + low estradiol in a woman <40 y. Early ovarian aging (A) has regular cycles with ↓ reserve; hypothalamic amenorrhea (C) has low/normal FSH, not elevated.

Q3. A 55-year-old woman on oral combined HRT develops a DVT. What is the best switch for managing her ongoing menopausal symptoms?

  1. Switch to higher-dose oral estrogen
  2. Switch to transdermal estrogen + progestogen
  3. Add raloxifene to current oral HRT
  4. Continue current therapy and add anticoagulation only
  5. Switch to raloxifene to maintain bone and vascular protection without VTE risk
Answer: B. Active/historical VTE is an absolute contraindication to oral estrogen; transdermal estrogen bypasses hepatic first-pass and carries no VTE/HTN risk, making it the preferred route. Increasing oral dose (A) worsens VTE risk. Adding raloxifene (C) also raises VTE risk. Continuing oral HRT (D) is contraindicated.

Q4. A postmenopausal woman not on HRT is found to have a DEXA T-score of −2.7. She cannot tolerate bisphosphonates. What is the next best option?

  1. Raloxifene — reduces vertebral fracture risk by 50% and does not affect TGs
  2. Tibolone — first-line for osteoporosis in all postmenopausal women
  3. Oral conjugated estrogen — first-line for osteoporosis prevention
  4. Subcutaneous calcitonin — preferred over all pharmacological agents
  5. Denosumab — first-line RANK-L inhibitor preferred when bisphosphonates fail in all postmenopausal women regardless of age
Answer: A. The sheets list raloxifene (SERM) as an alternative to bisphosphonates, reducing vertebral fracture risk by 50%, lowering LDL/total cholesterol without raising TGs. HRT (C) is not 1st-line for osteoporosis as risks outweigh benefits except in POI/premature menopause <50 y. Tibolone (B) is not listed as 1st-line for osteoporosis broadly.

Q5. A 49-year-old woman starts continuous combined HRT. At her 6-week follow-up she reports irregular spotting. What is the most appropriate response?

  1. Stop HRT immediately — any bleeding is abnormal
  2. Perform immediate endometrial biopsy
  3. Reassure — irregular spotting is expected for up to 3–6 months; review if it persists
  4. Switch to estrogen-only HRT to eliminate bleeding
  5. Switch to sequential combined HRT — cyclic withdrawal bleeds are more predictable than continuous regimens
Answer: C. Irregular spotting in the first 3–6 months of continuous combined HRT is normal as the endometrium becomes atrophic; it usually settles by 3–6 months. Immediate biopsy (B) is only warranted for persistent bleeding beyond 6 months or postmenopausal bleeding on investigation. Switching to estrogen-only (D) with an intact uterus is contraindicated.

Q1. A 45-year-old woman, P3, reports leaking urine every time she coughs or laughs. There is no urgency. Urodynamics show leakage with increased abdominal pressure and no detrusor contraction. What is the most accurate urodynamic diagnosis?

  1. Detrusor overactivity
  2. Overflow incontinence
  3. Urodynamic stress incontinence (USI)

  5. Functional incontinence due to impaired mobility
Answer: C. Leakage with ↑ abdominal pressure and no detrusor contraction on UDS = USI. Detrusor overactivity (A) would show a detrusor contraction at the moment of leakage.

Q2. A 55-year-old postmenopausal woman reports sudden strong urges to void, leaks before reaching the toilet, and gets up twice at night. She has no UTI on urinalysis. First-line medical treatment has failed. Which drug is most appropriate next?

  3. Mirabegron 50 mg
  4. Desmopressin 25 mcg
  5. Bethanechol 25 mg TDS (cholinergic agonist to increase detrusor tone)

Q3. A 60-year-old woman presents with a feeling of something coming down in the vagina. Examination reveals the entire uterus protruding through the introitus with skin ulceration. What degree of uterine prolapse is this, and what is the immediate local treatment for the ulceration?

  5. 3rd degree (procidentia); ring pessary insertion under general anaesthesia
Answer: B. Descent of the whole uterus through the introitus = 3rd degree / procidentia. Ulceration is treated with estrogen cream (heals within ~7 days) before definitive surgery.

Q4. A 38-year-old woman, P2, has stress urinary incontinence that has not responded to >3 months of supervised pelvic floor muscle training. She has no prolapse. Which surgical procedure is the most commonly used first-line option?

  1. Anterior colporrhaphy

Answer: C. The mid-urethral sling / TVT is the most commonly used surgical procedure for SUI (easy to learn, quick, outpatient). Anterior colporrhaphy (A) has only 50% continence success. Botox (D) is for OAB, not SUI.

Q5. A 70-year-old woman has a large cystocele and must digitally reduce the vaginal bulge to empty her bladder. Pelvic examination also shows she leaks with coughing. She is fit for surgery. What is the best surgical approach?

  2. Anterior colporrhaphy alone
